Draft 6: blank slate music theory

I recently picked up a guitar for the first time in a couple of years. I used to be a pretty decent player, but I’m a self-taught amateur with almost no understanding of formal music theory. As I’m starting again, I decided this would be my opportunity to learn “the proper way”.

I started with a bunch of research into music theory, trying to understand scales and chords and intervals and all these things. I found it so frustrating! My frustration had a specific flavour, not “this is hard to learn because it is a complicated topic”, but “this is hard to learn because it is being taught in a way that doesn’t suit my brain”.

So in this post I am searching for a different way of thinking about music, that suits my brain better than all the other explanations I’ve found. I might be reinventing the wheel, but it will definitely help me learn, and it might be useful to others too.

Principle: pitch is frequency

Most people find it easy to spot the difference between a very low rumbly bass sound and a very high squeaky soprano. Those different pitches are produced by different frequencies. When you hear a low sound, your eardrums are vibrating at a lower frequency than when you hear a high-pitched sound.

The frequency is measured in cycles-per-second, or Hertz (Hz). If I pluck the lowest string on a guitar, it will oscillate at 82Hz, physically wobbling back and forth 82 times in a second. If I pluck the highest string it will wobble at about 330Hz. That faster vibration is what we interpret as a high pitch.

Principle: music is made by avoiding most of the pitches

In general, our ears can pick up on the range from about 20Hz to 20,000Hz. There’s an infinite variety of possible pitches between 20 and 20,000Hz, because it is a continuous scale: 78.05Hz sounds different to 78.37Hz.

We find most combinations of pitches ugly, but a few of them sound beautiful (because math). To construct a musical system, the first job is to remove almost all of the possible pitches and create a small set that can be recombined in ways that sound good to us.

In Western music we’ve chosen a set of pitches that are used all over the world. By international agreement we have a thing called “concert pitch”, which tells us that the 49th key of an 88-key piano should be tuned to 440Hz. You could take an orchestra and re-tune all the instruments to 431.2Hz instead and most people wouldn’t notice the difference. But if you have one instrument playing at 431.2 while everyone else is playing at 440, you’ll get an ugly mess. That’s why we have the agreement: 440 is a kind of arbitrary choice, but it is helpful that everyone uses it.

The set of pitches we’ve chosen is a compromise between pure mathematical principles, our subjective experience of sound (psychoacoustics), and the physical limitations of constructing musical instruments. Other cultures have chosen different pitch sets, which can sound jarring to Western ears.

The compromises of Western music make it easier to learn how to play some instruments, but for a brain like mine, they actually make it harder to learn how music itself works. So what I am trying to do with this article is unlearn the confusing aspects and find a different way to explain how music is constructed, that suits my brain better.

Principle: octaves sound good

An octave is a mathematical concept: it’s a 2:1 relationship between two frequencies. It’s the interval or distance between two pitches where one is twice as high, or half as high as the other.

Principle: 1 octave = 12 semitones

In Western music, we decided to divide the octave into 12 steps. (Compared to Balinese music for instance, which divides the octave into 5 or 7 steps.)

Each of these steps is called a semitone and the full set of 12 is the chromatic scale. With any scale, when you get to the end you loop back to the start. So when you take the 13th step in the chromatic scale, you’re back to 1 again, but you’ve moved to the next octave.

It’s crucial to understand that a semitone is the distance between two pitches, it’s an interval, a relationship between two points in the space of all possible pitches.
